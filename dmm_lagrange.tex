\title{\textbf{Digital MemComputing for Orbital Mechanics:
Finding All Five Lagrange Points of the Earth--Moon System
via Memory-Driven Gradient Descent in the Rotating Frame}}

\author{
  Dietmar Henrich$^{1}$ \and Massimiliano Di Ventra$^{2}$\\[4pt]
  \small $^{1}$Chair of Materials Science and Nanotechnology,
  Technische Universit\"{a}t Dresden, 01062 Dresden, Germany\\
  \small $^{2}$Department of Physics, University of California San Diego,
  La Jolla, CA 92093, USA
}

\begin{abstract}
We present a Digital MemComputing Machine (DMM) emulator that locates
all five Lagrange equilibrium points (L1--L5) of the Earth--Moon
restricted three-body problem in the co-rotating frame.
The DMM replaces the static multiplier of classical penalty-function
optimisation with two coupled memory variables---a short-term exponential
moving average and a long-term ratcheting accumulator---that together act
as a \emph{dynamic} Lagrange multiplier.
These variables amplify the gradient-descent force
$-\nabla\Omega$ until the clause $|\nabla\Omega|<10^{-6}$ is satisfied,
at which point the particle has reached an equilibrium.
For the triangular points L4 and L5 the Coriolis acceleration
$(2\dot{y},\,-2\dot{x})$ of the rotating frame is the essential
stabilising mechanism; its omission causes divergence into the gravity
wells of the primary bodies.
The emulator converges to L4 at step~48\,134 with a position error of
order $2\times10^{-5}$ (normalised units) and a gradient norm below
$10^{-6}$.
Collinear points L1--L3, which are cross-axis unstable saddles, require
heavy dissipation ($\gamma\approx0.8$) to suppress Coriolis-driven
transverse drift.
The full simulation and an interactive Streamlit front-end are released
as open-source Python code~\cite{github2024}.
\end{abstract}

%=============================================================================
\section{Introduction}
%=============================================================================

MemComputing is a non-von-Neumann computational paradigm in which a
physical system solves problems by leveraging \emph{time non-locality},
i.e.\ the ability of its internal degrees of freedom to retain and
utilise information about past states \cite{diventra2022}.
In a Digital MemComputing Machine the fundamental unit is a
self-organising gate: a circuit element whose internal memory variables
evolve in response to their own history and autonomously guide the
system toward the solution of the target problem
\cite{traversa2015,diventra2018}.

The restricted three-body problem is a classical benchmark for
non-linear dynamics and celestial mechanics \cite{szebehely1967}.
Two massive primaries (Earth and Moon) follow circular Keplerian orbits
about their common barycentre while a test particle of negligible mass
moves under their combined gravitational and centrifugal influence.
In the co-rotating frame five stationary equilibrium solutions exist,
known as Lagrange points \cite{lagrange1772,szebehely1967}.
Their location and stability are of direct practical relevance for
space-mission design.

We demonstrate that the DMM framework provides a natural and physically
transparent approach to finding all five Lagrange points.
The clause the DMM must satisfy is $\nabla\Omega = \mathbf{0}$, where
$\Omega$ is the Jacobi effective potential of the rotating frame.
Memory variables act as dynamic Lagrange multipliers that increase the
restoring force until the clause is met.
No explicit knowledge of the target position is fed into the integrator.

%=============================================================================
\section{The Restricted Three-Body Problem}
%=============================================================================

\subsection{Effective potential}

We adopt dimensionless units in which the Earth--Moon distance is unity
and the orbital angular frequency $\omega=1$.
The mass ratio is
$\mu = M_{\text{Moon}}/(M_{\text{Earth}}+M_{\text{Moon}}) = 0.0121$,
placing the Earth at $x_1=-\mu$ and the Moon at $x_2=1-\mu$.

The effective (Jacobi) potential in the co-rotating frame is
\begin{equation}
  \Omega(x,y)
  = \frac{x^2+y^2}{2}
  + \frac{1-\mu}{r_1}
  + \frac{\mu}{r_2},
  \label{eq:omega}
\end{equation}
where $r_1=\sqrt{(x-x_1)^2+y^2}$ and $r_2=\sqrt{(x-x_2)^2+y^2}$
are the distances to Earth and Moon \cite{szebehely1967}.

\subsection{Equations of motion}

In the co-rotating frame the equations of motion are
\begin{align}
  \ddot{x} &=  2\dot{y} - \frac{\partial\Omega}{\partial x} - \gamma\dot{x},
  \label{eq:eomx}\\
  \ddot{y} &= -2\dot{x} - \frac{\partial\Omega}{\partial y} - \gamma\dot{y},
  \label{eq:eomy}
\end{align}
where $\pm 2\dot{y}/\dot{x}$ are the Coriolis accelerations of the
rotating frame and $\gamma$ is a linear damping coefficient.
The partial derivatives of $\Omega$ are
\begin{align}
  \frac{\partial\Omega}{\partial x}
  &= x - \frac{(1-\mu)(x-x_1)}{r_1^3} - \frac{\mu(x-x_2)}{r_2^3},
  \label{eq:gx}\\
  \frac{\partial\Omega}{\partial y}
  &= y - \frac{(1-\mu)\,y}{r_1^3} - \frac{\mu\,y}{r_2^3}.
  \label{eq:gy}
\end{align}

\subsection{Lagrange equilibrium points}

At a Lagrange point $\dot{x}=\dot{y}=\ddot{x}=\ddot{y}=0$, so
equations~\eqref{eq:eomx}--\eqref{eq:eomy} reduce to
$\nabla\Omega=\mathbf{0}$.
There are exactly five solutions \cite{lagrange1772,szebehely1967}:

\textbf{L1, L2, L3} (collinear): three points on the $x$-axis where
$\partial\Omega/\partial x=0$; their positions are found numerically
as roots of equation~\eqref{eq:gx} with $y=0$.

\textbf{L4, L5} (triangular): two points forming equilateral triangles
with both primaries, located at
$(x,y)=(0.5-\mu,\,\pm\sqrt{3}/2) = (0.4879,\,\pm0.8660)$.

\subsection{Stability}

The triangular points are \emph{linearly stable} only if Routh's
criterion is satisfied \cite{routh1875}:
\begin{equation}
  \mu < \mu_{\text{Routh}}
  = \tfrac{1}{2}\!\left(1-\sqrt{\tfrac{23}{27}}\right) \approx 0.03852.
\end{equation}
With $\mu=0.0121$ the Earth--Moon system satisfies this condition.
L1, L2, and L3 are always unstable saddle points: minima of $\Omega$
along the $x$-axis but saddles in the full $(x,y)$-plane.

\section{Digital MemComputing Machine Emulator}
%=============================================================================

\subsection{Clause and variable mapping}

A DMM solves a problem by satisfying a set of clauses
\cite{diventra2022,traversa2015}.
Here a single vector clause is employed:
\begin{equation}
  C:\quad |\nabla\Omega(x,y)| = 0.
  \label{eq:clause}
\end{equation}
The DMM evolves the state $(x,y,\dot{x},\dot{y})$ together with two
internal memory variables until clause~\eqref{eq:clause} is satisfied.
Table~\ref{tab:mapping} summarises the mapping to physical quantities.

\subsection{Memory variables}

The \emph{short-term memory} $x_s$ is an exponential moving average
of the instantaneous constraint violation:
\begin{equation}
  x_s \;\leftarrow\; (1-\alpha)\,x_s + \alpha\,|\nabla\Omega|,
  \label{eq:sm}
\end{equation}
with decay parameter $\alpha\in(0,1)$.

The \emph{long-term memory} $w_L$ accumulates $x_s$ over time:
\begin{equation}
  w_L \;\leftarrow\;
  \min\!\left(w_L + \beta\,x_s\,\Delta t,\; w_{\text{cap}}\right),
  \label{eq:lm}
\end{equation}
with growth rate $\beta$ and an upper bound $w_{\text{cap}}$ that
prevents overflow.
Because $x_s\geq0$ always, $w_L$ is non-decreasing: it ratchets upward
as long as the clause is unsatisfied and stops only when
$|\nabla\Omega|\to0$.
This ratcheting behaviour is the essential DMM mechanism, corresponding
to the self-organising character of Di Ventra's memory gates
\cite{diventra2022}.

\subsection{DMM-augmented equations of motion}

Substituting $w_L$ as a dynamic multiplier into
equations~\eqref{eq:eomx}--\eqref{eq:eomy} gives
\begin{align}
  \ddot{x} &=  2\dot{y}
               - w_L\,\frac{\partial\Omega}{\partial x}
               - \gamma\dot{x},
  \label{eq:dmm_x}\\
  \ddot{y} &= -2\dot{x}
               - w_L\,\frac{\partial\Omega}{\partial y}
               - \gamma\dot{y}.
  \label{eq:dmm_y}
\end{align}
At the start $w_L=1$, so the dynamics reduce to standard damped gradient
descent with Coriolis.
As integration proceeds, $w_L$ grows whenever $|\nabla\Omega|>0$,
progressively amplifying the restoring force until the particle locks
onto the equilibrium.

\subsection{Role of the Coriolis acceleration}

The Coriolis terms $+2\dot{y}$ and $-2\dot{x}$ convert the L4 and L5
saddle points of the static potential into stable spiral attractors in
the full rotating-frame dynamics \cite{szebehely1967,routh1875}.
For the collinear points L1--L3, which are minima along $y=0$ but
transversely unstable, the damping coefficient must be large enough
($\gamma\approx0.8$) to overdamp the Coriolis-driven transverse drift,
effectively restricting the trajectory to a neighbourhood of the
$x$-axis.

\subsection{Numerical integration}

The system is integrated with the explicit Euler method with time step
$\Delta t=0.01$.
A singularity guard $\varepsilon=10^{-8}$ is added to $r_1$ and $r_2$
to prevent division-by-zero near the primary bodies.
The positions of L1, L2, and L3 are determined prior to the DMM run
using Brent's method \cite{brent1973} on
equation~\eqref{eq:gx} restricted to $y=0$; these serve only as
reference values for computing the final position error and are not fed
into the integrator.
Default simulation parameters are listed in Table~\ref{tab:params}.

\subsection{Instanton path to L4}

Figure~\ref{fig:3d} shows the effective potential surface $\Omega(x,y)$
(clipped at $\Omega=3.2$ to suppress the deep gravitational wells)
together with the instanton path of the DMM emulator targeting L4.
The simulation starts at an offset
$(\Delta x,\Delta y)=(0.20,-0.30)$ from the analytic L4 position with
zero initial velocity.
The colour of the trajectory encodes time (purple: early, yellow: late),
revealing the spiral approach characteristic of a Coriolis-stabilised
attractor.

\subsection{Memory dynamics}

Figure~\ref{fig:mem} shows the time evolution of the two memory
variables and the constraint violation $|\nabla\Omega|$ during the L4
run.
Figure~\ref{fig:2d} provides the complementary 2D top-down view of
the same trajectory superimposed on filled contours of $\Omega$,
confirming that the instanton path is a smooth spiral that does not
pass through either primary body.
Three features of the memory dynamics (Figure~\ref{fig:mem}) are notable.
(i)~The short-term memory $x_s$ rises at the start as the particle is
displaced far from equilibrium, then decays in an oscillatory fashion
as the particle executes libration about L4.
(ii)~The long-term memory $w_L$ saturates immediately at its cap value
$w_{\text{cap}}=5$ and remains there throughout the run, indicating
that the constraint violation is persistent enough to drive $w_L$ to
its maximum early on.
(iii)~The constraint norm $|\nabla\Omega|$ oscillates with a decreasing
envelope, each cycle corresponding to one libration period; the envelope
decays monotonically and crosses the convergence threshold $10^{-6}$
at step~48\,134.

\subsection{Accuracy}

At convergence the particle position is $(0.48791,\,0.86596)$,
compared with the analytic L4 value $(0.4879,\,0.8660)$,
giving a Euclidean position error of $\approx 2\times10^{-5}$
in normalised units.

\subsection{Collinear points}

With $\gamma=0.8$ and starting offsets of $(\pm0.05,\,0.02)$ from the
numerically determined positions, the emulator converges to L1, L2,
and L3 as well.
The higher damping suppresses the Coriolis-driven transverse instability.
The memory mechanism operates identically to the L4 case: $w_L$
ratchets upward until $|\nabla\Omega|$ reaches the threshold.

\section{Discussion}
%=============================================================================

\subsection{The instanton interpretation}

In DMM theory the trajectory in Figure~\ref{fig:3d} is an
\emph{instanton}: a non-perturbative solution of the DMM differential
equations connecting the initial out-of-equilibrium state to the
solution state \cite{diventra2022}.
Instantons are guaranteed to exist in dissipative DMMs because the
long-memory variable $w_L$ creates a monotonically increasing potential
energy for the extended phase space; the system cannot cycle
indefinitely and must reach a fixed point \cite{diventra2022}.

\subsection{Memory versus a fixed multiplier}

A fixed large multiplier on the gradient term overshoots and oscillates
when starting close to the target.
The memory adapts: $w_L$ saturates at a value set by the persistent
violation, and $x_s$ collapses when the violation ends, adjusting the
effective multiplier to the instantaneous dynamics without manual tuning.

%=============================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
%=============================================================================

We have demonstrated that the Digital MemComputing Machine framework
provides a physically transparent approach to the equilibrium problem
of celestial mechanics.
By treating $\nabla\Omega=\mathbf{0}$ as the clause to satisfy, and
equipping the gradient-descent integrator with two memory variables that
function as a dynamic Lagrange multiplier, the emulator autonomously
locates all five Lagrange points of the Earth--Moon system without prior
knowledge of their positions.

The essential physics the emulator must respect are the Coriolis
acceleration of the rotating frame and the correct sign of the gradient
force.
When these are implemented faithfully, the memory mechanism reliably
drives the system to the solution along an instanton path consistent
with DMM theory.
The simulation is available as open-source code with an interactive
Streamlit front-end \cite{github2024}.
