\title{Memory as Dissipation: A Curvature-Adaptive Dynamical Solver for the
Lagrange Points of the Restricted Three-Body Problem, with an N-body Stability
Cross-Check}

\author{Dietmar Henrich}
\affiliation{Chair of Materials Science and Nanotechnology,
Technische Universit\"{a}t Dresden, 01062 Dresden, Germany}

\author{Massimiliano Di Ventra}
\affiliation{Department of Physics, University of California San Diego,
La Jolla, CA 92093, USA}

\begin{abstract}
We construct a continuous dynamical system that locates the five Lagrange
equilibria of the planar restricted three-body problem from generic initial
conditions, and we use it as a controlled setting to ask a precise question:
\emph{which term in the equations of motion actually performs the
computation?} We first analyse the natural memcomputing-style ansatz in which a
monotonically growing memory variable $w_L$ multiplies the gradient force. We
prove---via an explicit kinetic-energy identity---that this memory is
\emph{dynamically inert}: a growing multiplier on a conservative force can only
exchange energy with the potential, never dissipate it, so it cannot drive the
system to an equilibrium. Numerically, setting the memory growth rate
$\beta\!=\!0$ leaves the results unchanged, and removing the explicit viscous
damping makes the system fail for every $\beta$.

We then show how to make memory genuinely load-bearing by folding it into the
\emph{dissipation} rather than the force: a memory variable $m$ that integrates
the constraint violation, $\dot m=\beta\lvert\nabla\Omega\rvert$, sets the
damping coefficient $\gamma_{\rm eff}=\gamma_0+\kappa\,m$. With $\gamma_0=0$ the
memory becomes the \emph{sole} source of dissipation; it supplies a monotone
Lyapunov-type functional and drives convergence where the inert formulation
fails ($0/5\!\to\!5/5$ on Earth--Moon). A curvature-adaptive correction current,
whose sign is read from the local potential curvature $\Omega_{yy}$, converts
the three collinear saddle points into attractors of the damped flow. We give
the full set of equations with the rationale for each term, report honest
robustness limits (all five points for mass ratios
$\mu\gtrsim 3\times10^{-6}$; degradation at $\mu\sim10^{-7}$ due to a
corotation-ridge degeneracy of the potential), and discuss the relationship to
genuine Digital MemComputing Machines.
Finally, as an independent dynamical cross-check, we forward-integrate clouds of
test particles in the time-dependent field of the Sun and planets and confirm
that the discovered equilibria carry the expected stability: the triangular
points librate on tadpole orbits (period $147.7$ vs.\ $147.9$\,yr theory for
Jupiter) while the collinear points disperse, in accordance with Routh's
criterion. Code:~\cite{github2026}.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction and scope}
%=============================================================================

Digital MemComputing Machines (DMMs) solve problems by mapping their solutions
onto the stable fixed points of a continuous dynamical system whose
\emph{memory} variables act as dynamic Lagrange multipliers, guiding generic
trajectories to a solution along instanton paths~\cite{diventra2022,
traversa2015,diventra2018}. A recurring difficulty when transcribing a physics
problem into such a system is to verify that the memory variables are doing the
computational work, rather than being carried along by a more conventional
mechanism (damping, line search, projection).

This paper uses the Lagrange points of the planar restricted three-body problem
(RTBP) as a transparent test bed for exactly this question. The RTBP has five
known equilibria---three collinear saddle points and two triangular
maxima~\cite{lagrange1772,szebehely1967}---so we can check, term by term,
\emph{which part of the dynamics localises them}. We deliberately present the
construction as an honest sequence:

\begin{enumerate}
\item the effective potential and the equilibrium condition
  (Sec.~\ref{sec:rtbp});
\item the natural memcomputing ansatz with a memory \emph{multiplier} on the
  force, and a proof that this memory is dynamically inert
  (Sec.~\ref{sec:inert});
\item the curvature-adaptive correction current that turns saddles into
  attractors (Sec.~\ref{sec:sigma});
\item the corrected formulation in which \emph{memory is the dissipation},
  making it genuinely load-bearing (Sec.~\ref{sec:memdiss});
\item an optional Newton refinement and the small-$\mu$ limitation
  (Sec.~\ref{sec:refine});
\item results and an honest robustness assessment (Sec.~\ref{sec:results}).
\end{enumerate}

Every equation below is the one actually integrated in the accompanying
code~\cite{github2026}; the numbers in Sec.~\ref{sec:results} are produced by
that code.

\section{The restricted three-body problem}\label{sec:rtbp}
%=============================================================================

We work in the co-rotating frame with normalised units: the two primaries are
separated by unit distance, the angular velocity of the frame is
$\omega=1$, and the mass ratio is
\begin{equation}
  \mu=\frac{M_2}{M_1+M_2}\in(0,\tfrac12],
\end{equation}
placing the primary $M_1$ at $(-\mu,0)$ and the secondary $M_2$ at $(1-\mu,0)$.
The Jacobi effective potential is
\begin{equation}
  \Omega(x,y)=\frac{x^2+y^2}{2}+\frac{1-\mu}{r_1}+\frac{\mu}{r_2},
  \label{eq:omega}
\end{equation}
with $r_1=\sqrt{(x+\mu)^2+y^2}$, $r_2=\sqrt{(x-1+\mu)^2+y^2}$. The first term is
the centrifugal potential of the rotating frame; the last two are the
gravitational wells of the primaries. Its gradient is
\begin{align}
  \partial_x\Omega &= x-\frac{(1-\mu)(x+\mu)}{r_1^3}-\frac{\mu(x-1+\mu)}{r_2^3},
  \label{eq:gx}\\
  \partial_y\Omega &= y-\frac{(1-\mu)\,y}{r_1^3}-\frac{\mu\,y}{r_2^3}.
  \label{eq:gy}
\end{align}
\emph{Rationale.} A Lagrange point is a relative equilibrium: in the rotating
frame the test particle is stationary, so all velocities and accelerations
vanish and the equilibrium condition reduces to the single vector clause
\begin{equation}
  \boxed{\;\nabla\Omega(x,y)=\mathbf 0\;}
  \label{eq:clause}
\end{equation}
which has exactly five solutions: the collinear points $L_1,L_2,L_3$ on $y=0$
and the triangular points $L_{4,5}=(\tfrac12-\mu,\pm\tfrac{\sqrt3}{2})$.

The local curvature of $\Omega$ in the transverse direction,
\begin{equation}
  \Omega_{yy}=1-\frac{1-\mu}{r_1^3}+\frac{3(1-\mu)y^2}{r_1^5}
              -\frac{\mu}{r_2^3}+\frac{3\mu y^2}{r_2^5},
  \label{eq:oyy}
\end{equation}
is the single most informative local quantity in this paper. On the axis
($y=0$) it reduces to $1-(1-\mu)/r_1^3-\mu/r_2^3$, which is \emph{negative} at
all three collinear points (e.g.\ for Earth--Moon, $\mu=0.0121$:
$\Omega_{yy}=-4.15,-2.19,-0.011$ at $L_1,L_2,L_3$) and \emph{positive}
($+2.25$) at $L_{4,5}$. Thus $\operatorname{sign}\Omega_{yy}$ distinguishes
saddles from curvature-maxima using only information available at the current
point---no knowledge of the solution locations
(Fig.~\ref{fig:curv}).

\section{The natural ansatz, and why its memory is inert}\label{sec:inert}
%=============================================================================

\subsection{Memory as a multiplier on the force}

The natural memcomputing transcription introduces a long-term memory $w_L\ge1$
that grows while the clause~\eqref{eq:clause} is violated and multiplies the
gradient force, together with a Coriolis term ($\pm2\dot y,\mp2\dot x$) inherited
from the rotating frame and a viscous damping $-\gamma\dot{\mathbf r}$ added for
convergence:
\begin{align}
  \ddot x &= 2\dot y - w_L\,\partial_x\Omega - \gamma\dot x, \label{eq:nx}\\
  \ddot y &= -2\dot x - w_L\,\partial_y\Omega - \gamma\dot y, \label{eq:ny}\\
  \dot w_L &= \beta\,\lVert\nabla\Omega\rVert, \qquad w_L(0)=1.
  \label{eq:wmem}
\end{align}
\emph{Intended rationale.} Equation~\eqref{eq:wmem} is a ratchet: because
$\lVert\nabla\Omega\rVert\ge0$, the memory $w_L$ is non-decreasing, growing while
the clause is unsatisfied and halting only when $\nabla\Omega\to\mathbf0$. The
hope is that the growing multiplier amplifies the restoring force until the
trajectory is driven into an equilibrium, with $w_L$ playing the role of a
dynamic Lagrange multiplier.

\subsection{The memory does no work: an energy identity}

This hope is not borne out. Write the kinetic energy
$T=\tfrac12(\dot x^2+\dot y^2)$. Using \eqref{eq:nx}--\eqref{eq:ny},
\begin{equation}
  \dot T=\dot x\,\ddot x+\dot y\,\ddot y
   = \underbrace{2(\dot x\dot y-\dot y\dot x)}_{=\,0\ \text{(Coriolis)}}
     -\,w_L\,\dot{\mathbf r}\!\cdot\!\nabla\Omega
     -\gamma\lVert\dot{\mathbf r}\rVert^2 .
  \label{eq:Tdot}
\end{equation}
Two facts follow immediately. First, the Coriolis force does \emph{no work}:
its contribution $2(\dot x\dot y-\dot y\dot x)$ vanishes identically. Second,
the memory enters only through the term $-w_L\,\dot{\mathbf r}\!\cdot\!\nabla\Omega$,
which is the rate of work of a \emph{conservative} (curl-free) force scaled by
$w_L$. Such a term can only shuttle energy between kinetic and potential
reservoirs; it has no definite sign and integrates to a change in the (rescaled)
potential, not to dissipation. The \emph{only} negative-definite term in
\eqref{eq:Tdot} is the viscous one, $-\gamma\lVert\dot{\mathbf r}\rVert^2$.

\begin{proposition}[Memory inertness]\label{prop:inert}
Along \eqref{eq:nx}--\eqref{eq:wmem} with $\gamma=0$, a growing multiplier
$w_L$ cannot remove kinetic energy. For the reduced transverse oscillator
$\ddot y=-w_L\,k\,y$ with $k>0$ fixed and $\dot w_L=\beta\lvert k y\rvert\ge0$,
the energy $E=\tfrac12\dot y^2+\tfrac12 w_L k y^2$ obeys
\begin{equation}
  \dot E=\tfrac12\,\dot w_L\,k\,y^2\;\ge\;0 ,
\end{equation}
so the memory ratchet \emph{adds} energy; the apparent shrinking of the
amplitude $y_{\max}\sim\sqrt{2E/(w_Lk)}$ is due solely to stiffening of the
well, while $T$ does not decay.
\end{proposition}

\noindent The consequences, all confirmed numerically (Sec.~\ref{sec:results}):
with damping present the results are \emph{independent of $\beta$}
($\beta=0$ and the nominal $\beta=10^{-3}$ give identical outcomes, and the
multiplier never departs from $w_L\approx1$ by more than $0.2\%$ on the
convergence timescale); with $\gamma=0$ the system fails to converge for every
$\beta$. \emph{The computation in \eqref{eq:nx}--\eqref{eq:wmem} is done by the
viscous damping, not by the memory.} This is the central negative result that
motivates the rest of the paper.

\section{Curvature-adaptive correction current}\label{sec:sigma}
%=============================================================================

Before fixing the memory we must fix a second problem: even with damping, plain
gradient descent cannot settle on a saddle. At a collinear point with a small
transverse displacement $y>0$ one has $\Omega_{yy}<0$, hence
$\partial_y\Omega<0$, so the descent force $-\partial_y\Omega>0$ pushes the
particle \emph{away} from the axis. We therefore flip the sign of the
transverse force wherever the curvature is negative, defining the correction
current through
\begin{equation}
  \sigma=
  \begin{cases}
    +1, & \Omega_{yy}<0 \quad\text{(saddle: correction current)},\\
    -1, & \Omega_{yy}\ge0 \quad\text{(maximum: standard descent)},
  \end{cases}
  \label{eq:sigma}
\end{equation}
and replacing the transverse force $-\partial_y\Omega$ by
$+\sigma\,\partial_y\Omega$. \emph{Rationale.} Where $\Omega_{yy}<0$, setting
$\sigma=+1$ makes the transverse force $+\partial_y\Omega<0$ for $y>0$: a
\emph{restoring} force pulling the particle back to $y=0$. Where
$\Omega_{yy}>0$, $\sigma=-1$ recovers ordinary descent, which already restores.
The sign is computed from \eqref{eq:oyy} at the current position, so no
information about which equilibria are saddles is supplied externally. The
$\sigma$-flip converts every critical point of $\Omega$ into a stable rest point
of the damped flow; numerically, disabling $\sigma$ makes trajectories bound for
$L_4$ fail. The correction current is thus the curvature-adaptive ingredient
that makes the equilibria reachable at all---but, by Proposition~\ref{prop:inert},
it still requires a genuine dissipation channel to remove energy.

\section{Memory as dissipation}\label{sec:memdiss}
%=============================================================================

\subsection{Equations of motion}

The fix suggested by the energy identity~\eqref{eq:Tdot} is to let the memory
control the \emph{dissipation}---the only term that can remove kinetic
energy---instead of the conservative force. We introduce a scalar memory $m\ge0$
that integrates the clause violation and set the damping coefficient to grow
with it:
\begin{subequations}\label{eq:dmm}
\begin{align}
  \ddot x &= 2\dot y - \partial_x\Omega - \gamma_{\rm eff}\,\dot x,
  \label{eq:vx}\\
  \ddot y &= -2\dot x + \sigma\,\partial_y\Omega - \gamma_{\rm eff}\,\dot y,
  \label{eq:vy}\\
  \dot m &= \beta\,\lVert\nabla\Omega\rVert,\qquad m(0)=0,\quad m\le m_{\rm cap},
  \label{eq:m}\\
  \gamma_{\rm eff} &= \gamma_0+\kappa\,m .
  \label{eq:geff}
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
Here $\sigma$ is the correction current~\eqref{eq:sigma}, $\beta$ the memory
growth rate, $\kappa$ the memory--damping gain, $\gamma_0$ an optional baseline
damping, and $m_{\rm cap}$ a saturation bound. The gradient force now enters
with unit gain (the inert multiplier $w_L$ is removed entirely).

\subsection{Rationale, term by term}

\textbf{(i) Why memory now computes.} Substituting \eqref{eq:geff} into the
energy identity gives
\begin{equation}
  \dot T = -\,\dot{\mathbf r}\!\cdot\!\nabla_{\!\sigma}\Omega
           \;-\;(\gamma_0+\kappa\,m)\,\lVert\dot{\mathbf r}\rVert^2 ,
  \label{eq:Tdot2}
\end{equation}
where $\nabla_{\!\sigma}\Omega=(\partial_x\Omega,\,-\sigma\,\partial_y\Omega)$.
The memory $m$ now multiplies $\lVert\dot{\mathbf r}\rVert^2$, a
\emph{negative-definite} term: it directly removes kinetic energy. Memory has
moved from the one channel that cannot dissipate (the conservative force) to the
one that can (the velocity coupling).

\textbf{(ii) Lyapunov role and the ratchet.} Because
$\lVert\nabla\Omega\rVert\ge0$, equation~\eqref{eq:m} makes $m$ monotone
non-decreasing; it grows precisely while the clause is violated and stops
($\dot m\to0$) exactly at a solution. Its closed form,
\begin{equation}
  m(t)=\beta\!\int_0^t\!\lVert\nabla\Omega(\mathbf r(\tau))\rVert\,d\tau ,
  \label{eq:mint}
\end{equation}
is the accumulated $L^1$ violation along the path: a monotone, bounded
(by $m_{\rm cap}$) functional that supplies ever-stronger dissipation until the
motion is quenched at an equilibrium. This is the Lyapunov-type structure that
the inert multiplier lacked.

\textbf{(iii) The pure case $\gamma_0=0$.} Setting $\gamma_0=0$ makes memory the
\emph{sole} source of dissipation: at $t=0$ the flow is undamped, and braking
appears only as $m$ accumulates from the violation history. This is the clean
test of whether memory computes. Numerically (Sec.~\ref{sec:results}), with
$\gamma_0=0$ the inert formulation finds $0$--$1$ of $5$ points while the
present one finds all $5$. A nonzero $\gamma_0$ may be reinstated purely to
accelerate the initial transient, trading the conceptual purity of
``memory-only dissipation'' for speed.

\textbf{(iv) Why scalar, not per-axis, memory.} Equation~\eqref{eq:m} uses the
full gradient norm $\lVert\nabla\Omega\rVert$, damping both axes equally. A
per-axis variant $\dot m_i=\beta\lvert\partial_i\Omega\rvert$ underdamps the
transverse direction at collinear points, where $\lvert\partial_y\Omega\rvert$
is small on the axis; the trajectory then oscillates transversely and misses
$L_2,L_3$. The scalar choice avoids this and is what the code uses.

\subsection{Initial-condition grid}

No solution coordinates are supplied. We launch $N=100$ trajectories from a
$10\times10$ grid: ten $x$-values formed from four uniform points in
$[-1.4,1.4]$ plus six anchors at $x_{L_k}\pm\delta$
($\delta=\max(0.5\,r_H,0.02)$, $r_H=(\mu/3)^{1/3}$ the Hill radius) bracketing
the narrow collinear basins; ten $y$-values comprising three points in
$[-1.2,-0.15]$, a fine layer $\{-0.05,0,0.05\}$, and four points in
$[0.15,1.2]$. Starts inside singularity disks around either primary are removed.
Each trajectory is integrated with explicit Euler, $\Delta t=0.01$, until
$\lVert\nabla\Omega\rVert<10^{-4}$ or $2\times10^5$ steps. Default control
parameters: $\gamma_0=0$, $\kappa=1$, $\beta=0.5$, $m_{\rm cap}=10$
(Table~\ref{tab:params}).

\section{Newton refinement and the small-$\mu$ limit}\label{sec:refine}
%=============================================================================

\textbf{Refinement.} Because the five Lagrange points are the \emph{only} exact
zeros of $\nabla\Omega$, the converged endpoints may optionally be polished by a
few Newton iterations on $\nabla\Omega=\mathbf0$ (using the analytic Hessian),
snapping each to machine precision. Distinct polished zeros are then clustered;
the number of clusters is the number of equilibria discovered, with no solution
coordinates used as input. This refinement should fall back to the raw endpoint
if a Newton step fails to converge---which is exactly what happens in the
small-$\mu$ regime described next.

\textbf{Corotation-ridge degeneracy.} As $\mu\to0$, $\Omega\to\tfrac12 r^2+1/r$
($r=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$), whose gradient $r(1-1/r^3)$ vanishes along the
\emph{entire} unit circle $r=1$, not at isolated points. For finite but tiny
$\mu$ the secondary lifts this degeneracy only by $\mathcal O(\mu)$, so
$\lVert\nabla\Omega\rVert\lesssim\mathcal O(\mu)$ all along $r=1$. Two
consequences follow. (a)~Any fixed threshold $\lVert\nabla\Omega\rVert<10^{-4}$
is met everywhere on the ridge once $\mu\lesssim10^{-4}$, so trajectories halt on
spurious ridge points rather than localising to $L$-points. (b)~The Hessian is
near-singular along the ridge, so Newton refinement is ill-conditioned and often
fails there---which is why the polish must fall back rather than reject. This is
a property of the RTBP potential at extreme mass ratios, not an artefact of the
solver; it bounds the method's reliable range (Sec.~\ref{sec:results}).

\subsection{Memory as dissipation is load-bearing}

Table~\ref{tab:memdiss} reports the present formulation
\eqref{eq:vx}--\eqref{eq:geff} with $\gamma_0=0$, so that memory is the only
dissipation. Where the multiplier system finds $0$--$1$ points without external
damping, memory-as-dissipation finds all five, with $94/100$ trajectories
converging. The memory is therefore necessary for convergence: it is doing the
computation.

Figure~\ref{fig:energy} makes the mechanism explicit. For a single initial
condition we plot the kinetic energy $T(t)$ under both formulations with no
external damping. Under the multiplier ansatz ($\gamma=0$) $T$ stays
$\mathcal O(1)$ and even grows---consistent with Proposition~\ref{prop:inert},
the memory ratchet pumps energy in and the trajectory never settles. Under
memory-as-dissipation ($\gamma_0=0$) the same initial condition relaxes: the
memory $m$ and the dissipation $\gamma_{\rm eff}=\kappa m$ it generates ramp up
from zero as the violation accumulates (right panel), driving $T$ monotonically
to zero and $\lVert\nabla\Omega\rVert$ below threshold.

\section{Dynamical stability cross-check}\label{sec:stability}
%=============================================================================

The solver of Sec.~\ref{sec:memdiss} \emph{locates} the equilibria; the
correction current $\sigma$ makes all five stable fixed points of the
\emph{damped} extended flow, irrespective of their intrinsic stability. A
separate and stronger question is whether the discovered points are
\emph{dynamically} stable for a real test particle---i.e.\ whether a body placed
there stays. We answer it with an independent method that uses \emph{no} memory
and \emph{no} correction current: direct forward integration of the
gravitational $N$-body equations.

\subsection{Method}

Massless test particles are integrated in the heliocentric field of the Sun plus
the planets, the latter on prescribed circular, coplanar orbits at their mean
motions (units AU, yr, $M_\odot$, so $GM_\odot=4\pi^2$). The acceleration is
\begin{equation}
  \ddot{\mathbf r} = -\frac{GM_\odot\,\mathbf r}{r^3}
   + \sum_i GM_i\!\left[\frac{\mathbf r_i-\mathbf r}{|\mathbf r_i-\mathbf r|^3}
   - \frac{\mathbf r_i}{r_i^3}\right],
  \label{eq:nbody}
\end{equation}
where the bracket's second term is the \emph{indirect} term---the reflex
acceleration of the Sun, which is heliocentric and indispensable: without it the
Lagrange points are not equilibria of \eqref{eq:nbody} at all. A subtlety in
seeding the collinear points is that their normalized roots are
\emph{barycentric} (Sun at $-\mu$); the heliocentric distance is
$a\,|x_L+\mu|$, not $a\,|x_L|$ (an $a\mu\approx7.4\times10^5$\,km offset for
Jupiter). Equation~\eqref{eq:nbody} is integrated with velocity Verlet.

The integrator passes three validation tests on the Sun--Jupiter system: a
particle seeded exactly at $L_4$ stays stationary in the co-rotating frame (drift
$<10^{-4}$\,AU over $2000$\,yr); the Jacobi constant is conserved to a relative
$\sim10^{-8}$; and the residual $\lVert\ddot{\mathbf r}+n^2\mathbf r\rVert$ at the
seeded collinear points falls from $\sim10^{-2}$ (with the wrong, barycentric
radius) to $\sim10^{-12}$ (with $a|x_L+\mu|$), confirming true equilibrium.

\subsection{Results}

Figure~\ref{fig:stab} summarises a Sun--Jupiter run. Clouds seeded near $L_4$
execute \emph{tadpole} librations and stay co-orbital; the measured
small-amplitude period, $147.7$\,yr, matches the analytic value
$T_{\rm J}/\sqrt{27\mu/4}=147.9$\,yr to $0.1\%$~\cite{murray1999}. Clouds seeded
at the collinear points disperse: $L_1$ and $L_2$ escape within a single orbital
period (large positive Lyapunov exponent), while $L_3$---a much weaker
saddle---erodes slowly, retaining a majority only because its instability
$e$-folding time is long. The surviving fraction over $1.5\times10^4$\,yr is
$\sim95\%$ for $L_4$, $0\%$ for $L_1/L_2$, and a slowly declining $\sim70\%$ for
$L_3$.

\begin{figure*}[!t]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=0.96\textwidth]{fig_stability.pdf}
  \caption{%
    Independent $N$-body stability cross-check (Sun--Jupiter).
    \textbf{Left}: a cloud seeded near $L_4$ in the frame co-rotating with
    Jupiter; green paths are surviving \emph{tadpole} librations about $L_4$,
    grey are escapees. Sun, Jupiter, and the $L_3/L_4/L_5$ points are marked.
    \textbf{Right}: surviving fraction vs.\ time for clouds seeded at
    $L_4,L_1,L_2,L_3$. $L_4$ is stable; the collinear $L_1,L_2$ escape within
    one orbit; $L_3$ is also unstable but with a long $e$-folding time. No memory
    or correction current is used---this is pure gravitational integration.}
  \label{fig:stab}

This is exactly the behaviour predicted by the curvature classification used by
the solver: the triangular points ($\Omega_{yy}>0$) are linearly stable provided
$\mu<\mu_{\rm Routh}=0.03852$~\cite{murray1999}, satisfied by all systems in
Table~\ref{tab:solar} except Pluto--Charon ($\mu=0.11$, where $L_4/L_5$ are
$\nabla\Omega=0$ solutions but not dynamically stable); the collinear points
($\Omega_{yy}<0$) are saddles and dynamically unstable. The forward integration
thus confirms \emph{dynamically} the stability type that the solver reads
\emph{locally} from the sign of $\Omega_{yy}$, and explains why real bodies
populate $L_4/L_5$ (e.g.\ the Jupiter Trojans) but not the collinear points.

%=============================================================================
\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}
%=============================================================================

\textbf{What is and is not memcomputing here.} The lesson of
Sec.~\ref{sec:inert} is sharp and general: a memory variable that multiplies a
conservative force is computationally inert, because it cannot change the energy
budget in a definite direction. To make memory load-bearing it must couple to a
dissipative channel. Equations~\eqref{eq:vx}--\eqref{eq:geff} do this by letting
the accumulated-violation memory $m$ set the damping; with $\gamma_0=0$ the
memory is the sole dissipation and is provably necessary for convergence. We
regard this as a minimal, transparent instance of a memory variable performing
computation, and as a cautionary template for transcribing other problems into
DMM form: \emph{check the energy identity}.

\textbf{Relation to formal DMMs.} In the established DMM framework the memory
variables possess their own dynamics that endow the system with a Lyapunov
function and topologically protected instanton trajectories~\cite{diventra2022}.
Equation~\eqref{eq:m} provides a monotone functional~\eqref{eq:mint} and a
genuine dissipative role, but it is a single scalar controlling a viscous
coefficient rather than the full constraint-resolving memory structure of a
universal DMM. A complete Lyapunov analysis and the extension to vector,
constraint-coupled memories are natural next steps; the present construction
shows the minimal requirement (dissipative coupling) that any such memory must
satisfy.

\textbf{Comparison with classical solvers.} Newton and Brent locate a single
$L$-point in $\mathcal O(10)$ evaluations given a bracket or a close guess; they
are far faster per point but require prior knowledge of where each solution is
and, for the collinear points, of the symmetry $y=0$. The present system finds
all five from a generic grid without such input, at the cost of $10^4$--$10^5$
evaluations per trajectory. Its purpose is not to outrun root-finding on a
problem whose solutions are already bracketed, but to serve as a controlled
setting in which the computational role of memory can be isolated and tested.

%=============================================================================
\section{Conclusion}
%=============================================================================

We presented, with full equations and rationale, a curvature-adaptive
dynamical solver for the Lagrange points of the restricted three-body problem,
and used it to answer a precise question about memcomputing. A memory that
multiplies the gradient force is dynamically inert---a fact we proved with an
energy identity and confirmed numerically ($\beta$-independence with damping,
failure without it). Folding the same memory into the dissipation instead,
$\gamma_{\rm eff}=\gamma_0+\kappa m$ with
$\dot m=\beta\lVert\nabla\Omega\rVert$, makes it load-bearing: with $\gamma_0=0$
the memory is the sole dissipation and drives convergence to all five points
($0/5\to5/5$ on Earth--Moon), while a curvature-adaptive correction current
$\sigma=\operatorname{sign}(-\Omega_{yy})$ renders the saddle points reachable.
The method recovers all five equilibria for mass ratios
$\mu\gtrsim3\times10^{-6}$ and degrades predictably at $\mu\sim10^{-7}$ owing to
a corotation-ridge degeneracy of the potential, which we characterise rather
than conceal. Code and an interactive implementation are available
at~\cite{github2026}.
