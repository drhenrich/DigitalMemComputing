\begin{abstract}
	The MemComputing paradigm of computation employs time non-locality (memory) to
	solve a given problem by mapping its solutions into equilibrium points of a dissipative dynamical
	system. The problem is then solved not in the original state space of variables,
	but in an augmented phase space of original variables plus memory degrees
	of freedom. Although such an approach has been primarily used to solve
	combinatorial optimization problems, here we show that it can also be employed in the
	field of celestial mechanics. As an important example we introduce a MemComputing
	machine that discovers all five Lagrange equilibrium points of any restricted
	three-body system in the solar system, with mass ratios spanning eight orders of magnitude, without prior knowledge of their location.
	The dynamical system follows equations of motion in the co-rotating frame where memory degrees of freedom dynamically learn and modify the local character of the phase space landscape guiding the system towards the equilibria. We finally discuss this approach vis-\`a-vis existing methods.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction and scope}\label{sec:intro}
%=============================================================================

MemComputing is a non-von-Neumann paradigm of computation in which a physical system exploits
\emph{time non-locality} (memory) to solve a given 
problem~\cite{diventra2022}. Here, memory is not synonymous with storage, rather it expresses the fact that the 
system retains information about its past, and exploits such information to perform useful computation. 
In this paradigm, the solutions of the target
problem are mapped onto the stable fixed points of a continuous dissipative dynamical system,
so that trajectories launched from generic initial conditions are guided to the
solution along specific paths~\cite{bearden2020efficient,diventra2018}. The computation
proceeds in an augmented phase space whose coordinates are the original
variables \emph{plus} the memory degrees of freedom. To date this paradigm has
been applied mostly to combinatorial optimization where input and output are binary~\cite{diventra2022,traversa2017}. However, the approach can be extended and applied to other 
equally important computational problems. 

Here, we show that MemComputing can be easily adapted to 
solve a paradigmatic continuous problem of celestial mechanics: the restricted three-body problem~\cite{szebehely1967}. 
The restricted three-body problem (RTBP) consists in two massive primaries on a circular
orbit and a massless test particle. It is a cornerstone of nonlinear dynamics and
of practical importance for space mission design~\cite{szebehely1967,Koon}. Its five equilibria, known as the
Lagrange points~\cite{lagrange1772}, are normally located by analytic or
numerical root finding that require a separate bracket or initial guess for each
point, and prior knowledge of the solution structure. 

In this paper, we construct instead a
single MemComputing machine that discovers all five Lagrange points of \emph{any} restricted
three-body system in the solar system from a generic grid of initial conditions,
without being given the coordinates of any solution. The machine is an autonomous dissipative dynamical system in the co-rotating
frame with memory degrees of
freedom that keep track of the local curvature of the phase space and {\it dynamically} transform saddle points of 
the original problem into 
minima of the expanded phase space, while leaving the original minima undisturbed. This way, the machine is always guaranteed to converge to one 
of the Lagrange points. We verify it on all
twenty-three Sun--planet and planet--moon pairs, spanning mass ratios from
$\mu \approx 2\times10^{-9}$ to $\mu \approx 0.1$, list every parameter needed to
reproduce the results, and close by comparing the approach with existing methods. It is our hope that this example will inspire the community to employ the MemComputing paradigm in solving other celestial mechanics problems.

\section{The restricted three-body problem}\label{sec:rtbp}
%=============================================================================

As it is commonly done~\cite{szebehely1967,Koon}, we use the co-rotating frame in normalized units: the two primaries of masses $M_1$ and $M_2$ are separated by
unit distance, the orbital angular velocity of the frame is $\omega=1$, and the mass
ratio is
\begin{equation}
  \mu = \frac{M_2}{M_1+M_2}\in(0,\tfrac12],
\end{equation}
placing $M_1$ at $(-\mu,0)$ and $M_2$ at $(1-\mu,0)$.  The Jacobi effective potential experienced by the massless test particle at position $(x,y)$ is the sum of the gravitational potentials of the two primary masses plus the centrifugal potential within the rotating reference frame:
\begin{equation}
  \Omega(x,y)=\frac{x^2+y^2}{2}+\frac{1-\mu}{r_1}+\frac{\mu}{r_2},
  \label{eq:omega}
\end{equation}
with $r_1=\sqrt{(x+\mu)^2+y^2}$ and $r_2=\sqrt{(x-1+\mu)^2+y^2}$. This is the
negative of the Jacobi potential commonly written with overall minus signs. With the
convention~\eqref{eq:omega} the Lagrange points are the critical points of $\Omega$, namely those points in space at which the gradient of $\Omega$ evaluates to zero: 
$\nabla\Omega(x,y)=(0,0)$.

From Eq.~(\ref{eq:omega}) the gradient is
\begin{align}
  \partial_x\Omega &= x-\frac{(1-\mu)(x+\mu)}{r_1^3}-\frac{\mu(x-1+\mu)}{r_2^3},
  \label{eq:gx}\\
  \partial_y\Omega &= y-\frac{(1-\mu)\,y}{r_1^3}-\frac{\mu\,y}{r_2^3}.
  \label{eq:gy}
\end{align}
%\begin{equation}
%  \;\nabla\Omega(x,y)=\mathbf 0\;
%  \label{eq:clause}
%\end{equation}
The critical point condition then provides exactly five solutions: the collinear points $L_1,L_2,L_3$ (which do not have a closed-form analytical expression) on the axis $y=0$ and the
triangular points $L_{4,5}=(\tfrac12-\mu,\pm\tfrac{\sqrt3}{2})$~\cite{szebehely1967,Koon}.

The \emph{nature} of each critical point---which is what the machine must
contend with---is set by the Hessian of $\Omega$. Its transverse ($y$) component is
\begin{equation}
  \Omega_{yy}=1-\frac{1-\mu}{r_1^3}+\frac{3(1-\mu)y^2}{r_1^5}
              -\frac{\mu}{r_2^3}+\frac{3\mu y^2}{r_2^5}.
  \label{eq:oyy}
\end{equation}
On the axis ($y=0$) this reduces to $1-(1-\mu)/r_1^3-\mu/r_2^3$, which is
\emph{negative} at all three collinear points. Together with the fact that the $x$ component of the Hessian 
$\Omega_{xx}=1+2(1-\mu)/r_1^3+2\mu/r_2^3>0$ and $\Omega_{xy}=0$ there, the collinear
points are \emph{saddles}, unstable in the transverse ($y$) direction. At the
triangular points one finds $\Omega_{xx}=\tfrac34$, $\Omega_{yy}=\tfrac94$, and
$\Omega_{xy}=\tfrac{3\sqrt3}{4}(1-2\mu)$, giving $\det[\mathrm{Hess}\,\Omega]=
\tfrac{27}{4}\mu(1-\mu)>0$ with positive trace: $L_4,L_5$ are \emph{minima} of
$\Omega$~\cite{note}. The sign of $\Omega_{yy}$ thus distinguishes the two cases: it is negative
in the unstable direction of the collinear saddles and positive at the triangular
minima (Fig.~\ref{fig:landscape}). 

The presence of saddle points means that a dynamical system 
approaching them would be repelled by the unstable manifold and unable to really find their position. What is then 
needed is a machine that ``learns'' the curvature of the Jacobi potential and smoothly converts the saddle points into 
equilibria, while keeping the equilibria unaffected. All this using only information generated along its own
trajectory. This is the essence of the MemComputing approach where the solutions are then the stable fixed
points of the autonomous flow in the augmented space of the original variables plus memory degrees of freedom.

\section{Memory-induced phase-space engineering}\label{sec:inversion}
%=============================================================================

The equations of motion of this MemComputing machine can be easily determined. To the ordinary rotating-frame equations of motion---the gradient force $-\nabla\Omega$ and the Coriolis
acceleration---we add a viscous term (to make the dynamical system dissipative, hence supporting attractors) and introduce a memory degree of freedom, $B$, which learns the local 
curvature of the Jacobi potential, and feeds into a second one, $s$, whose role is to change the index of the critical points accordingly. More precisely, $s$ 
is tasked to change the collinear saddle points to minima, while leaving the character of the triangular points untouched. 

The damped flow that descends $\Omega$ in the rotating frame is then, 
\begin{subequations}\label{eq:flow}
\begin{align}
  \ddot x &= 2\dot y-\partial_x\Omega-\gamma\dot x,\\
  \ddot y &= -2\dot x+s\,\partial_y\Omega-\gamma\dot y,
\end{align}
\end{subequations}
with constant viscosity $\gamma>0$ and the memory term $s$ multiplying the
transverse force. We will solve these equations using the simple forward Euler with a fixed time step~\cite{sauer2018numerical}.

Whenever $s$ approaches $-1$ the transverse force is the ordinary descent term
$-\partial_y\Omega$, which is restoring at a minimum. The flow then settles onto the
minima of $\Omega$, i.e., the triangular points $L_4,L_5$. At a collinear saddle,
however, the transverse direction has $\Omega_{yy}<0$: a small displacement $y>0$
gives $\partial_y\Omega<0$, so it pushes the particle
\emph{away} from the axis and the saddle repels. Having the memory approaching $s=+1$ reverses this single
term into a restoring force, converting the saddle's unstable direction into a
stable one---the saddle becomes a minimum of the effective, inverted landscape and
an attractor of the flow. The key point is that this memory term is intrinsically position-dependent, and no constant value of $s$ would work 
in the whole phase space.

\section{Learning the curvature}\label{sec:memory}
%=============================================================================

The adaptive rule of Sec.~\ref{sec:inversion} requires the sign of $\Omega_{yy}$ at every point in space.
Although reading it from the analytic Hessian~\eqref{eq:oyy} would work, this route would be unsatisfying:
the machine would be handed the very curvature information that distinguishes the
solutions. We instead let a memory degree of freedom \emph{learn} it. The flow
already evaluates the gradient $\mathbf g=\nabla\Omega$ at every step of the computation, and the
Jacobian of $\mathbf g$ is the Hessian. A memory matrix $B$ therefore accumulates a
\emph{quasi-Newton} estimate of the Hessian from successive gradient samples. Setting
$\Delta\mathbf p=\mathbf p_k-\mathbf p_{k-1}$, 
$\Delta\mathbf g=\mathbf g_k-\mathbf g_{k-1}$, and $\Delta t =t_k-t_{k-1}$, at steps $k$ and $k-1$ of the simulation, we can then update the matrix $B$ from step 
$k$ to step $k+1$ (call these matrices $B_k$ and $B_{k+1}$, respectively) with a
Broyden (quasi-Newton) secant update~\cite{broyden1965,nocedal2006} and a slow relaxation term 
toward the identity,
\begin{equation}
	B_{k+1}=B_k
	+\frac{(\Delta\mathbf g-B_k\,\Delta\mathbf p)\,\Delta\mathbf p^{\mathsf T}}
	{\Delta\mathbf p^{\mathsf T}\Delta\mathbf p}
	+\lambda\,(\mathbf{1}-B_k)\,\Delta t ,
	\label{eq:broyden}
\end{equation}
applied only whenever $\lVert\Delta\mathbf p\rVert$ exceeds a small threshold (otherwise
$B_{k+1}=B_k$). The result is then symmetrized at every step, by performing the operation $(B_{k+1}+B_{k+1}^{\mathsf T})/2$, to respect the symmetry of a
Hessian. The two added terms in Eq.~(\ref{eq:broyden}) 
are of different character. The first is the secant correction, which enforces
$B\,\Delta\mathbf p=\Delta\mathbf g$ along the current step: a full per-step
assimilation whose size is set by how wrong $B$ is and is \emph{independent} of
$\Delta t$ (Broyden is a discrete iteration, with no continuum limit as $\Delta t$
shrinks to zero). The second
is a genuine rate---a slow leak toward the identity. Because the curvature varies
across the landscape, this leak gives $B$ a finite memory horizon $\sim1/\lambda$, so
it tracks the \emph{local} curvature rather than a path-history average, and relaxing
toward $\mathbf{1}$ supplies a safe default ($B_{yy}=1$) where the step is too small to
measure. Equivalently, $B$ follows a continuous leak $\dot B=-\lambda(B-\mathbf{1})$
punctuated by discrete secant measurements---the structure of a continuous--discrete
(Kalman-type) estimator~\cite{nocedal2006}.
The estimated transverse curvature is the component $B_{yy}$, that enters the memory variable $s\in[-1,1]$ which relaxes toward the learned sign,
\begin{equation}
	\dot s = -\alpha\Bigl[\tanh\!\Bigl(\tfrac{B_{yy}-\theta_s}{\varepsilon_c}\Bigr)+s
	\Bigr].
	\label{eq:sdot}
\end{equation}
Here, the
threshold $\theta_s<0$ in~\eqref{eq:sdot} biases $s$ toward the safe descending
state $s=-1$, inverting only when the learned curvature is \emph{clearly} negative.
This protects the minima and the small-$\mu$ corotation ridge (where
$\lVert\nabla\Omega\rVert\approx0$ along $r=1$ and the curvature estimate hovers
near zero) while still inverting at genuine saddles, where $B_{yy}$ is strongly
negative. The bias is needed because the damped rotating frame is not symmetric
under $y\to-y$---the Coriolis term breaks it---so $L_4$ and $L_5$ have slightly
different basins. Therefore, biasing toward the safe state treats both robustly.

The parameter $\varepsilon_c$ controls the width of the window around $\theta_s$, namely how 
sharply $B_{yy}$ crossing the threshold triggers a change of index of the saddle points. Finally, 
$\alpha$ controls how fast the memory variable $s$ varies. It is chosen such that the relaxation time of $s$, $\sim 1/{\alpha}$ is short 
compared to saddle's instability time scale (of order one in the rotating-frame units).  

The augmented phase space is thus $(x,y,\dot x,\dot y,\,s,\,B)$: the 
mechanical variables plus the memories $(s,B)$. The full machine integrates
\eqref{eq:flow} with $\gamma$ constant, advancing $B$ by
\eqref{eq:broyden} and $s$ by \eqref{eq:sdot} at each step. No
analytic second derivative appears anywhere in the dynamics ($\Omega_{yy}$ may be used
only, after the fact, to confirm that the learned $B_{yy}$ is correct).

We note that the resulting scheme is \emph{predictive}. During the
off-axis approach to a saddle the trajectory samples gradients over a range of
positions, so $B$ accumulates a usable curvature estimate \emph{before} the
trajectory reaches the unstable region---it does not have to wait for the runaway to
develop. A purely reactive scheme, namely one that would infer instability from the growth of
the motion, would be outrun by the exponential instability of the saddle and fail. The
quasi-Newton accumulation is what makes the memory fast enough.

{\it Integration.}
As anticipated, the equations of motion are integrated by explicit forward Euler~\cite{sauer2018numerical} with fixed step $\Delta t$. A threshold 
$\varepsilon=10^{-9}$ is added to $r_1,r_2$ to avoid divergences. The memory is initialized at
$B=\mathbf{1}$, $s=-1$ (the safe descending state), and the Broyden
update~\eqref{eq:broyden} is applied only when $\lVert\Delta\mathbf p\rVert$ exceeds
a small threshold, so that near a fixed point---where $\Delta\mathbf p\to0$---the estimate
simply stops. A trajectory ends when the magnitude of the gradient is below a threshold ($\lVert\nabla\Omega\rVert<\theta$), when it
leaves the disk $\lVert\mathbf r\rVert>12$, or after $N_{\max}$ steps.

{\it Initial conditions grid.}
No solution coordinates are given to the dynamics. Starting positions are
generated from the \emph{generic structure} of the RTBP only, using the Hill radius
$r_H=(\mu/3)^{1/3}$ and the secondary position $x_{\rm s}=1-\mu$ as follows:
(i) on-axis seeds $(x_{\rm s}\pm c\,r_H,0)$ for
$c\in\{0.5,0.7,1,1.4,2,3,5,8\}$, bracketing the collinear $L_1,L_2$;
(ii) $L_3$ seeds near $x=-1$;
(iii) equilateral seeds about $(\tfrac12,\pm\tfrac{\sqrt3}{2})$ for the triangular
points;
(iv) a coarse off-axis fill for the broad large-$\mu$ basins.
Initial conditions inside the exclusion disks around either primary are discarded, leaving of
order seventy initial conditions per system. Even the equilateral initial conditions use the fixed
$(\tfrac12,\pm\tfrac{\sqrt3}{2})$ rather than the $\mu$-dependent solution.
%; the dynamics and the refinement below produce the exact positions.

\section{Advantages and limitations of this approach}\label{sec:discussion}

The MemComputing approach clearly finds all Lagrange points of every solar-system pair with just one set
of parameters. However, if we could identify a suitable volume around a Lagrange point, classical root finders, like the Newton method~\cite{sauer2018numerical},
would converge towards that point in a handful of evaluations, namely much faster than integrating a trajectory. This could be construed as a disadvantage
of this approach. The advantage with respect to
these traditional methods is then of a
different kind. The machine is \emph{global and prior-knowledge-free}: one autonomous
flow, one parameter set, locates all five equilibria of any system from a generic
grid, without a bracket per point and without being told which points are saddles.
More broadly, it finds critical points of \emph{any index}, not only minima---the
force inversion turns a saddle into an attractor---which ordinary gradient descent
and the optimization methods built on it cannot do.

Beyond Newton's method, several other approaches are used in practice to locate Lagrange points.
First, the triangular points $L_4, L_5$ are always at the exact closed-form positions
$(\frac{1}{2}-\mu,\pm\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2})$, so no numerical step is needed.
Second, the collinear points have no closed form but their locations can be expressed as
power series in $\mu^{1/3}$~\cite{szebehely1967}; for small mass ratios these series are
accurate enough to avoid further iteration.
Third, since $L_1,L_2,L_3$ all lie on the $x$-axis ($y=0$), a simple one-dimensional
bisection on $\partial_x\Omega$ locates each one in a few function evaluations, because
the gradient changes sign at every collinear point.
Finally, homotopy (continuation) methods track each equilibrium as $\mu$ varies from a
known value, following the solution branch smoothly to the target mass ratio.
All these methods are faster per point than the MemComputing dynamics when they
apply---but each requires either exact prior structural knowledge (the triangular closed
form, the collinear axis constraint) or a separate bracket or continuation path per
point. The MemComputing machine requires none of this: it discovers all five points
simultaneously from a generic grid, treating saddles and minima on equal footing. 

In the particular example of this paper, the inversion of the index of the critical point is a single
transverse sign because the collinear saddles' unstable direction is the $y$-axis. However, for a general situation, the same memory matrix $B$ supplies the unstable eigendirections, and the force would be inverted along them. We thus expect this approach could be generalized to other types of problems in orbital mechanics, where 
finding saddle points of arbitrary symmetry may be necessary.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
%=============================================================================

In conclusion, we have introduced a MemComputing machine that locates all five Lagrange points of
the restricted three-body problem by descending the Jacobi effective potential while 
inverting the force along the unstable direction of its saddles. The inversion sign
cannot be constant---no fixed choice captures both the saddles and the minima---so it
is carried by memory degrees of freedom, which learn the local curvature as
a quasi-Newton estimate accumulated from the machine's own gradient evaluations, with
no analytic second derivative in the dynamics. The machine discovers all five points
of every Sun--planet and planet--moon pair in the solar system---twenty-three systems with mass ratios ranging  
from $\mu\approx2\times10^{-9}$ to $0.1$---from a generic grid of initial conditions and a single
set of parameters. This problem extends the applicability of the MemComputing paradigm to celestial
mechanics and provides another example in which memory (time non-locality)
aids in the computation. We therefore hope that this work will inspire the use of the MemComputing paradigm in other studies of celestial mechanics.
